Teacher Salary Versus Cost of Living by State: Where Teachers Get Ahead in 2026

Teacher pay headlines are misleading. When a state announces a record-high average salary, it rarely mentions what that salary can actually buy. A teacher earning $92,000 in New York might have less purchasing power than one earning $55,000 in Alabama, once rent, groceries, and daily expenses are factored in. The nominal number on your contract tells you what the district pays. The adjusted number tells you how you actually live.

This analysis ranks all 50 US states by teacher salary adjusted for cost of living. We take each state's average teacher salary for the 2025-2026 school year and divide it by the state cost of living index (where 100 equals the national average). The result is a purchasing-power-adjusted salary that reveals where teachers genuinely get ahead financially and where high sticker-price salaries are an illusion.

Key Takeaways

  • Michigan ranks first in teacher salary purchasing power, with an adjusted salary of $77,628 thanks to a strong nominal salary and below-average living costs.
  • The Midwest dominates the top 10. Six of the ten best states for teacher purchasing power are in the Midwest, where moderate salaries stretch significantly further.
  • New York and California, the two highest-paying states by raw salary, drop to 22nd and 43rd after cost-of-living adjustment.
  • Hawaii is the worst state for teacher purchasing power. An average salary of $68,000 becomes just $35,251 in adjusted terms due to a cost of living index nearly double the national average.
  • The adjusted salary gap between the best and worst state is $42,377, more than many starting teacher salaries.

How We Calculated Adjusted Teacher Salary

The methodology is straightforward. For each state, we use the average public school teacher salary for the 2025-2026 school year, based on NEA estimates and state education department data. We then divide that salary by the state's cost of living index (sourced from the Missouri Economic Research and Information Center and the Bureau of Economic Analysis Regional Price Parities), which measures relative price levels where 100 is the national average. The formula:

Adjusted Salary = (Average Teacher Salary / Cost of Living Index) x 100

A state with an average teacher salary of $65,000 and a cost of living index of 85 produces an adjusted salary of $76,471. That teacher can buy as much with their $65,000 as someone earning $76,471 in a state at the national average. Conversely, a teacher earning $75,000 in a state with a cost of living index of 120 has an adjusted salary of just $62,500, meaning their higher paycheck delivers less real purchasing power.

Top 10 Best States for Teacher Purchasing Power

The top tier of this ranking is dominated by Midwestern and Mid-Atlantic states that combine solid teacher salaries with below-average costs of living. These are the states where a teaching career delivers the strongest financial foundation.

1. Michigan (Adjusted: $77,628)

Michigan leads the nation in teacher purchasing power. The state's average teacher salary of $69,400 is healthy but not headline-grabbing. What makes Michigan exceptional is its cost of living index of 89.4, meaning everyday expenses run about 10.6 percent below the national average. Housing in cities like Detroit, Grand Rapids, and Lansing remains remarkably affordable by national standards, with median home prices well under $250,000 in most metro areas. Teachers in Michigan also benefit from a comparatively strong pension system and union representation that has historically pushed compensation upward.

2. Illinois (Adjusted: $77,331)

Illinois is one of the few states where high nominal pay and moderate costs converge. The average teacher salary of $73,542 ranks in the top dozen nationally, and the statewide cost of living index of 95.1 keeps expenses below the national average. Outside of Chicago proper, Illinois offers genuinely affordable living. Even within the Chicago metro area, suburban districts often pay salaries exceeding $80,000 while offering access to housing that costs a fraction of what equivalent homes would run in the Northeast or West Coast.

3. Pennsylvania (Adjusted: $76,907)

Pennsylvania's combination of a $72,600 average salary and a 94.4 cost of living index places it third nationally. The state benefits from a wide range of affordable housing markets, particularly in the western half around Pittsburgh and in the central regions. Philadelphia pushes costs higher in the southeast, but even there, teacher salaries tend to be significantly above the state average, partially offsetting the urban premium.

4. Ohio (Adjusted: $75,783)

Ohio is a prime example of a state where a moderate salary delivers outsized value. At $65,780, the average teacher salary is close to the national average, but the 86.8 cost of living index amplifies its purchasing power dramatically. Cities like Columbus, Cleveland, and Cincinnati offer a full suite of urban amenities at costs that would be unrecognizable to teachers in coastal metros. A teacher buying a home in suburban Columbus pays roughly one-third of what the same square footage would cost in suburban Boston or San Jose.

5. New Jersey (Adjusted: $75,507)

New Jersey is the highest-cost state in the top 10, and it earns its position purely through salary. At $85,700, New Jersey pays teachers the third-highest nominal salary in the country. Despite a cost of living index of 113.5, that robust pay translates to genuine purchasing power. Teachers in southern New Jersey and the central parts of the state can access relatively affordable housing while still earning salaries calibrated to the state's overall high-cost reputation.

6-10: Minnesota, Connecticut, Wisconsin, Rhode Island, Iowa

Minnesota (adjusted $75,109) and Wisconsin (adjusted $74,275) represent the Upper Midwest's consistent strength in this ranking: decent pay paired with costs well below average. Minneapolis pushes Minnesota's cost of living higher than neighboring states, but the state average still comes in at 91.8. Connecticut (adjusted $74,750) mirrors the New Jersey model: a very high nominal salary of $82,300 that overcomes a 110.1 cost of living index through sheer pay scale. Rhode Island (adjusted $73,783) and Iowa (adjusted $73,415) round out the top 10, with Iowa offering perhaps the most striking value proposition in the entire ranking: a $60,200 salary that an 82.0 cost of living index transforms into the equivalent of over $73,000 in purchasing power.

Bottom 10: Where Teacher Salaries Fall Short

The bottom of the ranking reveals states where teachers face genuine financial strain, either because salaries are low, costs are high, or both.

50. Hawaii (Adjusted: $35,251)

Hawaii is the single worst state in the country for teacher purchasing power, and it is not close. The state's average teacher salary of $68,000 looks respectable on paper, but a cost of living index of 192.9, nearly double the national average, destroys its value. Housing is the primary driver: median home prices on Oahu exceed $800,000, and one-bedroom apartments in Honolulu regularly surpass $2,000 per month. Teachers in Hawaii frequently work second jobs or live with multiple roommates well into their careers. The gap between Hawaii and the 49th-ranked state (Oregon) is larger than the gap between most other adjacent ranks in the entire table.

49. Oregon (Adjusted: $58,895)

Oregon's $68,200 average teacher salary is above the national mean, but a cost of living index of 115.8 pulls adjusted pay below $59,000. Portland drives much of the elevated cost, with housing prices that have roughly doubled over the past decade. Teachers in rural Oregon face lower costs but also significantly lower pay than their Portland-area counterparts, leaving the statewide adjusted figure stubbornly low.

48. Colorado (Adjusted: $59,868)

Colorado has experienced one of the fastest cost-of-living increases in the country over the past decade. The state's appeal to remote workers and tech companies has driven Denver-area housing costs sharply upward, pushing the statewide index to 105.9. Meanwhile, teacher salaries have not kept pace. At $63,400, the average teacher salary is below many neighboring states with far lower living costs. Teachers in the Front Range corridor, from Fort Collins to Colorado Springs, face a particularly acute affordability squeeze.

47. Mississippi (Adjusted: $60,843)

Mississippi is the one state in the bottom 10 that ranks poorly purely because of low pay rather than high costs. The state's cost of living index of 81.8 is among the lowest in the nation, but an average teacher salary of $49,770, the lowest in the country, means even those low costs cannot compensate. A teacher in Mississippi has less purchasing power than one in Ohio despite living in a state that costs 5 percent less.

46. Arizona (Adjusted: $61,117)

Arizona's teacher pay crisis has been well documented. The state's average salary of $56,900 sits below the national average, while the cost of living index of 93.1 reflects the rapid growth of the Phoenix metro area. Phoenix's population boom has driven housing costs sharply upward without a corresponding increase in teacher compensation. The state has struggled to recruit and retain teachers, with vacancy rates consistently among the highest nationally.

45-41: Florida, New Mexico, California, Montana, Washington

Florida (adjusted $62,004) combines the sixth-largest population with below-average teacher pay of $56,300, while Miami's booming housing market pushes costs above average. New Mexico (adjusted $63,141) has raised teacher salaries in recent years but still lags behind regional peers. California (adjusted $63,272) is the most dramatic example of high pay failing to deliver: the state's $90,100 average salary ranks second nationally, but a 142.4 cost of living index, the second highest after Hawaii, means teachers in Los Angeles and San Francisco struggle with housing costs that consume 40 to 50 percent of take-home pay. Montana (adjusted $63,308) and Washington (adjusted $63,605) complete the bottom group, with Seattle's extreme housing costs dragging down Washington's otherwise strong salary figures.

The Biggest Surprises in the Data

Several patterns emerge from this analysis that challenge conventional wisdom about teacher compensation.

Low-Pay States That Outperform

Alabama ($56,340 salary, rank 20th), Oklahoma ($53,800 salary, rank 30th), and South Dakota ($52,400 salary, rank 31st) all rank in the top 31 despite paying teachers well below the national average. Their secret is cost of living indices in the low 80s or even upper 70s. A teacher in Oklahoma earning $53,800 has nearly identical purchasing power to a teacher in Nevada earning $62,200 because Oklahoma costs 15 percent less to live in. For teachers willing to live in states that rarely make national headlines for their pay scales, the math works out surprisingly well.

High-Pay States That Disappoint

New York ($92,222 salary, rank 22nd), California ($90,100 salary, rank 43rd), and Massachusetts ($88,900 salary, rank 14th) pay the highest nominal salaries in the country but deliver middling to poor purchasing power. New York's cost of living index of 134.5 absorbs the bulk of its salary advantage, and California's 142.4 index is even more punishing. A New York City teacher earning $95,000 has roughly the same buying power as a teacher in Indiana earning $60,500. Massachusetts fares somewhat better, with Boston's high costs partially offset by a salary that is nearly 30 percent above the national average.

The Georgia Anomaly

Georgia ranks 16th nationally with an adjusted salary of $71,171, a position that would surprise many education policy observers. The state has quietly built a competitive teacher salary structure ($63,200 average) while maintaining a cost of living index of 88.8. The Atlanta metro area pushes costs higher, but suburban Atlanta districts like Gwinnett and Cobb County pay salaries exceeding $65,000 while still offering affordable housing relative to other major metros. For teachers considering a move to a Sun Belt state, Georgia offers measurably better purchasing power than Florida, Arizona, or Colorado.

What This Means for Teachers Considering a Move

Relocation decisions for teachers involve more than salary and cost of living. Pension portability (or lack thereof), tenure requirements, state income tax rates, and certification reciprocity all factor in. But the purchasing power data in this analysis provides the financial foundation for any such decision.

A few practical takeaways for teachers evaluating a move:

  • Do not chase the highest nominal salary. New York, California, and Massachusetts pay the most but deliver 14th, 43rd, and 14th-best purchasing power respectively. The salary number on your offer letter matters far less than what it buys.
  • The Midwest is the most consistently strong region. Michigan, Illinois, Ohio, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa, Indiana, and Nebraska all rank in the top 17. If quality of life matters more than zip code prestige, these states deliver.
  • The Sun Belt is a mixed bag. Texas (21st) and Georgia (16th) perform well, but Florida (45th), Arizona (46th), and Colorado (48th) rank near the bottom. Rapid population growth has pushed housing costs up faster than teacher salaries have risen.
  • Small states can surprise. Wyoming (13th), Delaware (11th), and Rhode Island (9th) all punch above their weight in teacher purchasing power.

State Income Tax: An Additional Factor

This analysis adjusts for cost of living but not for state income tax, which can further shift the picture. Seven states have no income tax at all: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Among these, Texas (21st) and Wyoming (13th) already rank well for teacher purchasing power, and the absence of state income tax makes their positions even stronger in practice. Florida (45th) and Washington (41st), however, rank poorly despite the tax advantage because their costs of living have risen so sharply.

On the other end, high-tax states like California (state income tax up to 13.3 percent), New York (up to 10.9 percent plus NYC local tax), and Oregon (up to 9.9 percent) see their already-diminished purchasing power further reduced on a take-home basis. A teacher in California earning $90,100 with an effective state tax rate near 6 percent loses an additional $5,400 per year compared to a counterpart in Texas, on top of the already significant cost-of-living disadvantage.

The Teacher Pay Problem Is a Purchasing Power Problem

National conversations about teacher pay tend to focus on whether average salaries are rising or falling. That framing misses the point. Teacher pay has increased in most states over the past five years, but cost of living has increased faster in many of the states that pay the most. The result is a widening gap between nominal compensation and real purchasing power.

The states at the top of this ranking are not necessarily the ones investing the most in education. They are the ones where the economic environment allows teacher salaries to translate into genuine financial stability: affordable housing, reasonable grocery and transportation costs, and a cost structure that lets a teacher build savings, buy a home, and plan for retirement without needing a second income or a partner's salary.

For individual teachers, the message is simple: where you teach matters at least as much as what you earn. A $55,000 salary in Iowa buys a better life than a $90,000 salary in California. The numbers are not opinions. They are arithmetic.
